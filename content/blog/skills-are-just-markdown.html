Skills look trivial. A Skill can be “just a markdown file” that contains instructions to prime an AI. It feels like a lightweight feature, not a technological shift.

That reaction misses the point.

The file format does not matter. The leverage comes from what the markdown represents: a portable package of expert problem-solving that a machine can execute on demand. Once you can publish, discover, and reuse those packages at scale, you stop treating expertise as something locked inside people and institutions. You start treating it as infrastructure.

This essay argues a simple claim: Skills look easy, but they create the conditions for a new knowledge economy. The economy does not emerge from better prompts. It emerges from distribution, discovery, incentives, and trust.

A Skill Is Not “Just a Prompt”

Yes, a Skill can be a prompt. In practice, a useful Skill encodes more than instructions. It encodes the parts of expertise that non-experts usually cannot improvise:

  • Problem framing: what questions to ask, what signals matter, what to ignore.
  • Workflow: step-by-step procedures, checklists, decision trees, templates.
  • Tool use: what tools to call, what inputs to request, how to validate outputs.
  • Constraints: jurisdiction rules, definitions, safety boundaries, escalation triggers.
  • Quality standards: rubrics, expected output formats, verification steps.

If you compress expertise into a single sentence, you get this: experts win because they explore the right solution space quickly. A Skill can encode that exploration strategy.

That changes the role of the AI. The AI stops acting like a generic chat partner. It starts acting like a specialist interface that runs an expert’s playbook.

What Becomes Possible: Standing on Expert Shoulders

People already “stand on the shoulders of giants” through books, courses, and open source code. Skills can make that standing interactive, personalized, and immediate.

A non-expert often fails for one reason: they do not know what to ask. They also do not know which intermediate steps matter. Skills fill that gap by forcing structure onto messy situations. They guide the user through the same sequence an expert would follow.

This is the practical promise:

  • An amateur can do higher-quality work because the system supplies expert framing and procedure.
  • The user does not need the vocabulary or mental models that normally gatekeep the domain.
  • The AI does not just output an answer. It leads a process.

Be careful with the strongest version of the claim. Skills will not magically turn amateurs into experts in high-stakes domains. They can still miss context, misinterpret inputs, and fail without real-world verification. But they can raise the floor dramatically in many domains, especially where the work is procedural, document-based, or pattern-driven.

The real shift is this: when you standardize expert reasoning into reusable Skill packages, you can distribute competence the way software distributes capability.

Discovery Is the Real Bottleneck

Most “prompt libraries” failed because users could not find the right prompt at the right time. They had to know what they needed before they could search for it.

Skills can only matter if a platform solves discovery. Two discovery problems exist:

1) User discovery: “What Skill do I need?”

Users describe symptoms, business problems, or vague goals. They do not know the name of the domain that will solve it. A good system should infer the domain and recommend the right Skill with high precision.

2) Skill discovery: “Which version is trustworthy?”

Once a user enters a category, they face spam, clones, and low-quality variants. The platform must rank by demonstrated performance, not by marketing.

If you do not solve discovery, Skills remain niche tools used by power users. If you solve discovery, Skills become ambient infrastructure. The platform quietly routes each user to the best expert workflow for their situation.

That is when “just markdown” becomes a distribution channel for expertise.

Publishing and Distribution Turn Skills Into a Market

Discovery makes Skills usable. Publishing makes them scalable.

Once creators can publish Skills into a registry, and users can activate them instantly, the system starts to resemble an app ecosystem. Except the product is not an interface. The product is a chunk of expertise.

This creates second-order effects:

  • Experts can specialize further because distribution expands their audience.
  • Teams can standardize internal workflows by codifying “how we do it here.”
  • Organizations can build Skill stacks that reflect their standards, risk posture, and domain constraints.

At that point, the key question stops being “Can you write a better prompt?” and becomes “Can you build the supply chain for expertise?”

Incentives Decide Whether Experts Participate

Most experts will not publish high-quality Skills for free at scale.

A real Skill requires cognitive labor. The creator must formalize tacit knowledge, expose their reasoning to scrutiny, and maintain it over time. Maintenance matters because domains change, tools change, and best practices shift.

So you need incentives that pay for:

  • initial creation
  • verification and review
  • ongoing updates
  • incident response when something goes wrong

A workable “skill economy” can use a mix of models:

Pay-per-use royalties

Users pay a small amount per invocation or per successful outcome. The platform shares revenue with the creator and reviewers. This fits domains where the Skill substitutes for paid professional time.

Subscriptions and bundles

Users pay for curated packs: contract analysis, sales enablement, data cleaning, interview prep, compliance checks. Bundles reduce search friction and fund maintenance.

Bounties

A company or institution funds a Skill that meets published test criteria. This pushes creators to build measurable quality.

Sponsorship for open Skills

A sponsor funds an open Skill because broader access benefits them. This matches education, public interest, and foundational knowledge.

Maintenance and review rewards

Pay the people who keep Skills accurate. If you only pay creators, the ecosystem degrades.

Incentives do not just solve participation. They determine the shape of the ecosystem. A platform that cannot compensate expertise at the right level will fill with shallow content.

The Hard Question: Paid Skills and Source Code

If users pay to use a Skill, should they see the Skill “source code” (the markdown)?

You can design this in several viable ways. Each trades off trust, defensibility, and usability.

1) Open source

Users can inspect, fork, self-host, and improve. This maximizes trust and accelerates iteration. It shifts monetization to hosting, certification, support, sponsorship, and reputation.

2) Source available with restrictions

Users can read the Skill but cannot redistribute or compete. This improves trust while protecting the creator’s business.

3) Closed source but auditable

Users do not see the code, but qualified reviewers can. The platform publishes measurable evidence: test results, update history, provenance, and risk rating. This fits high-stakes domains where users want accountability but creators want protection.

4) Fully closed black box

Users see only behavior. This produces the lowest trust and highest scam risk. It can work in low-stakes domains or when the publisher brand already carries trust.

A serious platform should not treat this as a philosophical debate. It should define minimum transparency requirements for all paid Skills, even closed ones:

  • behavior spec: what it does and what it refuses
  • versioning and changelog
  • evaluation results on a published harness
  • provenance metadata: assumptions, jurisdiction, references, last updated
  • escalation triggers for high-stakes situations

If you cannot prove quality, you cannot justify a marketplace.

Open Skills, a Commons, and the “Wikipedia for Skills”

Could Skills become a wiki? Yes, but not the naive version.

Wikipedia works because low-risk knowledge tolerates broad editing. Many Skills operate in high-stakes contexts where a wrong step can cause harm. So you need a commons with stronger gates:

  • open contribution, but tiered permissions
  • credential checks for protected domains
  • mandatory tests before publish
  • peer review workflows
  • rollback, deprecation, and version pinning
  • clear ownership and stewardship for maintenance

Think of it as “Wikipedia mechanics plus software engineering discipline.” Treat each Skill like a living artifact with CI, tests, and release notes. Treat contributors like maintainers, not authors.

This also creates a powerful hybrid: open Skills as the base layer, paid Skills as premium layers, and institutional sponsorship to keep essential Skills free.

Risks You Cannot Ignore

A skill economy will attract misuse and low-quality production. You need controls early.

Quality collapse

Without tests and review, the marketplace becomes prompt spam. Users lose trust fast. Platforms must rank by demonstrated performance and verified provenance.

Staleness

Skills decay as domains change. Platforms must enforce update cadences and flag stale Skills. They should show “last reviewed” as prominently as price.

Misuse

Some Skills can enable harm. Platforms need policy, monitoring, and controlled access for dual-use content. They must classify risk and enforce stricter guardrails for high-risk categories.

Overreliance

Users will treat Skills as authority. Platforms must design for humility: clear limits, required context checks, and escalation to professionals when necessary.

If you do not build risk controls, regulators and enterprises will block adoption. If you overcorrect, you kill the ecosystem. Platforms must balance access and safety with explicit governance, not vibes.

The Core Argument: Infrastructure Creates the Economy

Skills are small artifacts. The big deal is not the markdown.

The big deal is that Skills can become the unit of exchange for expertise if you build:

  1. Discovery: routing users to the right Skill at the right moment
  2. Distribution: publishing, versioning, composition, and bundling
  3. Incentives: payments that fund creation, review, and maintenance
  4. Trust: provenance, evaluation, credentialing, and auditability
  5. Risk controls: classification, guardrails, and incident response

Once those exist, you get a new kind of knowledge economy:

  • people pay to use other people’s expertise as packaged workflows
  • experts earn from reuse rather than repeated one-to-one delivery
  • open commons Skills raise the baseline for everyone
  • specialized paid Skills fund deep work and continuous maintenance

That is why Skills matter. They turn expert cognition into distributable infrastructure. The file looks simple because the value lives in the ecosystem around it.
