The most dangerous part of an election is not always the voting. It is often the journey from the polling unit to the final declaration.

At the polling unit, everyone can see the ballots being counted. Party agents can watch the numbers being written down. Observers can photograph the result sheet. Citizens nearby can compare what they saw with what was announced.

Then the result begins to move.

It moves from the polling unit result sheet to ward collation. From ward collation to local government collation. From local government collation to state collation. From state collation to national collation. At each step, the numbers are copied, added, transferred, announced, disputed, defended, and sometimes corrected.

INEC's own voter-education material describes collation as the tabulation or summation of votes, and it lists multiple collation levels depending on the election: ward, local government, constituency, senatorial district, state, and national levels. It also warns that problems during collation and declaration can compromise public confidence in an otherwise well-run election.

That is the problem this diagram is trying to reason about.

The current weak point

The diagram starts with ballot papers becoming a polling unit result sheet, usually Form EC8A. That result sheet is the first structured record of the vote count. It is the source that later collation should preserve, not reinterpret.

BVAS already creates a bridge between the physical polling-unit result and the digital record by capturing the result image for upload to IReV, the INEC Result Viewing Portal. That is useful because it gives citizens, parties, journalists, and observers a way to see polling-unit result images outside the collation room.

But image publication alone does not fully solve the collation problem. If the enforceable result path still runs through manual transfer from one form to another, the system still depends on repeated human copying at the exact stage where incentives to manipulate are highest.

The risk is simple: a correct polling-unit result can be photographed, uploaded, and still be weakened later if the structured numbers used for collation are manually re-entered without a hard reconciliation requirement.

The product question

The diagram asks a better product question: what if BVAS did not only capture the image of the result sheet, but also captured the structured electronic figures on the same device, at the same polling unit, before the result enters the long collation chain?

That turns BVAS from an image-transfer device into the first structured data-entry point for results. The polling unit would produce two linked artifacts:

  • the result-sheet image, visible to the public;
  • the structured party scores, accreditation count, rejected ballot count, and other required figures, validated before submission.

The important design rule is that both artifacts must describe the same event. The image is the human-readable evidence. The structured record is the machine-readable data. They should be captured together, signed together, timestamped together, and published together.

NERER as the source of truth

The diagram labels the destination as the National Electronic Register of Election Results. The name matters less than the principle: Nigeria needs a public, append-only electronic register of polling-unit results that later collation levels must reconcile against.

In that model, ward, local government, state, and national collation do not become invisible manual transformations. They become aggregation views over polling-unit records that are already public.

That changes the posture of collation. Instead of asking citizens to trust that the numbers were carried faithfully through each level, the system lets citizens recompute the totals themselves. A ward total can be checked against its polling units. A local government total can be checked against its wards. A state total can be checked against its local governments. The national total can be checked against the states.

The collation room still exists. Legal declaration still matters. Disputed ballots and cancelled polling units still need official handling. But the arithmetic becomes less mysterious because the base data is public early enough for everyone to inspect it.

What this would prevent

This kind of pipeline is not a magic cure for election fraud. It would not stop vote buying. It would not stop intimidation. It would not solve poor logistics. It would not make a bad polling-unit process good.

It targets a narrower failure mode: result manipulation after counting.

If every polling-unit result is published as both image and structured data, later changes become easier to detect. If a party's votes shrink between the polling unit and ward collation, the public record shows where the difference began. If an LGA total does not match the sum of its wards, the mismatch is visible. If a state total disagrees with the published LGA records, the contradiction is not buried inside a PDF released after the fact.

The point is not that software should replace the law. The point is that software should make the lawful process easier to verify.

The hard requirements

A real system would need more than a portal and a database. It would need strict process design.

First, the polling-unit submission must be atomic. The image, structured scores, device identity, polling-unit identifier, timestamp, accreditation count, and officer credentials should be submitted as one record. Splitting those into separate workflows creates room for mismatch.

Second, the system should validate arithmetic before accepting the record. Total votes should not exceed accredited voters. Party scores should add up consistently with valid votes. Required fields should not be optional. INEC has publicly described newer BVAS safeguards that move in this direction by requiring direct score entry and internal validation checks before upload.

Third, publication should be fast and open. If the data only becomes public after the political battle has moved on, it is evidence, but it is not a strong deterrent. The register should expose polling-unit records, aggregation summaries, update history, and machine-readable downloads.

Fourth, corrections must be explicit. There will be genuine errors: mistyped numbers, poor images, cancelled units, court orders, and disputed forms. The system should not silently overwrite records. It should publish a correction trail showing the original record, the corrected record, the legal basis, the official who approved it, and the time of change.

Fifth, the public interface should not be an afterthought. IReV helped because ordinary people could inspect result images. A national electronic register should go further: searchable polling units, downloadable CSV files, signed result bundles, collation-level dashboards, and APIs for civic groups and media houses.

The real argument

This is not primarily a technology argument. It is an accountability argument.

Manual collation asks citizens to accept a chain of custody they cannot fully observe. A public electronic register shortens that trust chain. It does not eliminate trust, but it moves trust from private rooms to public evidence.

That is the standard Nigerian elections should be moving toward: every polling-unit result captured once, validated at source, published in real time, collated transparently, and preserved with an audit trail.

The job of election technology is not to make democracy feel modern. It is to make manipulation harder, detection faster, and evidence available to everyone.
